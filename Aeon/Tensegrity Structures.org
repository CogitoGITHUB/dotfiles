* Overview
Tensegrity (tensional integrity) structures rely on a balance of continuous tension and isolated compression. They are lightweight, resilient, and self-stabilizing.

* Key Concepts
** Compression
- Elements: rods, struts
- Discontinuous: do not touch each other directly
- Bear compressive forces

** Tension
- Elements: cables, tendons, wires
- Continuous: connect all compression elements
- Bear tensile forces

** Stability
- Achieved via global equilibrium of tension and compression
- No rigid joints required

* Properties
- Lightweight but strong
- Flexible and resilient
- Self-stabilizing
- Modular and scalable
- Efficient material usage

* Examples
** Architecture & Design
- Sculptures and art installations
- Modern architectural roofs
- Example: Kurilpa Bridge (Australia)

** Space Structures
- Deployable antennas and solar arrays
- NASA tensegrity robots for planetary exploration

** Robotics
- Robots that crawl, roll, or deform to adapt to terrain

** Biology
- Cells, cytoskeleton modeling
- Bones as compression struts, muscles/fascia as tension

** Everyday Objects
- Furniture: chairs, tables, light fixtures
- Artistic tensegrity pieces (Kenneth Snelson)

* Advantages
- Challenging traditional structural norms
- Energy and material efficiency
- Dynamic adaptability
- Suited for extreme environments
